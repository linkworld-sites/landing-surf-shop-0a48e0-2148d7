The internet will tell you to watch YouTube videos, practice on the sand, and take a lesson from a certified instructor. That advice isn't wrong — but it's incomplete.

Here's what nobody says out loud:

## The first time is going to feel impossible

Your first paddle out will probably end before it begins. The white water will push you back. Your arms will burn after ten minutes. You'll swallow salt water, get your board knocked into you, and wonder why anyone does this voluntarily.

That's normal. That's all of us.

The surfers you see making it look effortless have been doing this for years. They got knocked around too. The difference is they showed up the next day.

## Start in the white water

Before you even think about the lineup, learn to ride the foam. White water — the broken wave pushing in toward shore — is the best teacher on earth. It's consistent, forgiving, and endlessly available.

Spend your first two sessions just in the foam. Get comfortable with the pop-up. Get comfortable falling. Get comfortable paddling hard and committing.

## The right board matters more than anything else

A beginners biggest mistake is grabbing a board that's too small. Shortboards look cool in magazines. They also require years of experience to paddle into a wave.

If you're learning, you want volume. Lots of it. A foam softboard in the 8 to 9 foot range will catch waves you didn't know existed. You'll stand up more. You'll have more fun. Your arms won't give out before you get the hang of it.

Every single board in our rental rack is sized for progression — we'll put you on what actually fits your size and experience, not what looks good in a photo.

## Read the ocean before you paddle

Spend five minutes on the beach before you go in. Watch how the waves are breaking. Where's the channel? Where do surfers return to the lineup without fighting through the break? Where are the biggest sets?

That five minutes of observation will save you thirty minutes of exhausted paddling in the wrong direction.

## The lineup has rules — and they exist for good reasons

Once you get past the break, there's an etiquette to surfing that might feel complicated from the outside. In practice, it comes down to two things:

**The surfer closest to the peak of the breaking wave has priority.** If someone is already up and riding, stay out of their way.

**Don't snake.** Paddling around someone to gain priority on a wave is bad form and can get you hurt.

Beyond that, be patient, be respectful, and don't apologize for being a beginner. Everyone in that lineup was where you are.

## Come back tomorrow

The most important session isn't the first one. It's the second one.

The first time, your body is in shock. Your muscle memory doesn't exist yet. Your instincts are all wrong. You're thinking about everything consciously — where your feet go, when to stand, how to angle the board.

The second time, something clicks. Not everything. But something.

The third time, more clicks. By the time you've had a handful of sessions, your body starts to understand what your brain is still trying to figure out.

See you in the water.
