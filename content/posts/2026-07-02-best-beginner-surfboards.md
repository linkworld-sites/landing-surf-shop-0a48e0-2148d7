## What Actually Makes a Good Beginner Surfboard

Before the board-by-board breakdown, here's what to look for. Three things matter above everything else:

**Volume.** Volume (measured in liters) is how much the board floats you. More volume means easier paddling, easier pop-ups, more waves caught. As a beginner, you want 80–120L minimum. Don't let anyone talk you down from that.

**Length.** Longer boards are more stable. More stable boards let you focus on technique instead of just trying not to fall. You want at least 7'6" to start, and 8'6"–9'6" if you've never surfed before.

**Width and thickness.** A wide, thick board has more surface area and floats better. This is less intuitive than length, but a wide 8'0" will often be easier to learn on than a narrow 9'0". Both dimensions matter.

## 5 Best Surfboards for Beginners in 2026

### 1. Classic Foam Softboard — 8'0"–9'0"

This is the one. If you've never surfed before, this is what you want under your feet.

Softboards — sometimes called foamies — have a foam deck and soft, flexible fins. This means you won't hurt yourself when the board hits you (and it will hit you), they paddle incredibly well for their size, and you'll catch twice as many waves as you would on a hard board.

The downside? They're not the most photogenic boards in the lineup. But here's the thing: you'll be standing and riding while the person next to you on a performance board is struggling to even get into waves. Coolness is relative.

**Best for:** Absolute beginners, first 5–10 sessions<br/>
**Difficulty:** Easiest

### 2. Traditional Longboard — 9'0"–9'6"

The longboard is the board surfing was born on, and it remains one of the best learning tools ever made.

A 9'6" log gives you maximum stability, maximum paddle power, and a very forgiving ride. It's slower to turn than a shorter board — but when you're learning, "slow to turn" means "stable and forgiving," which is exactly what you want. Longboarders tend to build fundamentals that stick with them forever.

**Best for:** Beginners who want to build long-term technique<br/>
**Difficulty:** Easy

### 3. Mini Mal (Malibu) — 7'6"–8'0"

The mini mal sits in the sweet spot between a full longboard and a performance shortboard. At 7'6"–8'0", it's easier to carry than a 9'6", while still offering enough volume and length to be genuinely beginner-friendly.

Mini mals are the go-to rental board for a reason: they work for a huge range of skill levels, catch waves easily, and are stable enough for beginners without feeling like a barge. If you're shopping for your first board, this is where most people land.

**Best for:** Beginners ready to progress, anyone who's had a few sessions<br/>
**Difficulty:** Easy–Medium

### 4. Funboard / Hybrid — 7'0"

At 7'0", the funboard straddles the line between beginner and intermediate. It has the volume and width of a learner board, but at this length you'll start developing real turning technique.

We'd recommend this as a second board rather than a first — or for beginners who are reasonably athletic and pick things up quickly. If you've had 5–10 sessions on a softboard or mini mal and you're catching waves consistently, a funboard is a great next step.

**Best for:** Progressing beginners, surfers with a strong sports background<br/>
**Difficulty:** Medium

## Should You Rent or Buy a Surfboard?

This is the question we get every single day at the shop. Here's our honest answer: **start by renting.**

Renting lets you try different boards without committing. You might think you want a longboard and discover you love a mini mal. You might show up on a small day and realize you need more foam under you. Renting a few times before buying is almost always the right call.

**When should you buy?** When you're surfing at least once a week, you've found a board type you consistently love, and you're spending enough on rentals to justify the investment.

A good beginner surfboard costs $300–$600 new, or $150–$300 used. If you're renting for $25–$30/session, you hit break-even somewhere in the 10–20 session range.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What size surfboard is best for a beginner?**

For most beginners, an 8'0"–9'0" foam softboard or longboard is the right call. The goal is maximum volume and maximum length. Don't size down too early — more board means more waves, and more waves means more fun and faster learning.

**Can I learn to surf on a shortboard?**

Technically, yes. Practically, no. A shortboard requires paddle fitness, pop-up speed, and wave-reading ability that takes years to develop. Learning on a shortboard is like learning to drive on a Formula 1 car. Get comfortable on a softboard or longboard first — your technique will thank you later.

**Should I buy a used surfboard for learning?**

Used boards can be great value — but inspect them carefully. Look for dings, delamination (where the fiberglass separates from the foam), and cracks near the fins. Small dings are easily repaired. A board that's been repaired multiple times may be waterlogged and heavy.

**What's the difference between a foamie and a hard board?**

A foam softboard (foamie) has a soft foam deck and flexible fins — safer and more forgiving for beginners. Hard boards (fiberglass or epoxy) have a harder, glassed surface that's more responsive but less forgiving. Beginners should almost always start on a foamie or a longboard.
